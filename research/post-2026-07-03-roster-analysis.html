The Bombers' Quarterback Carousel: Dru Brown Returns as Winnipeg Recalculates

The Brown Trade: A Costly Correction

The Blue Bombers' acquisition of quarterback Dru Brown from the Ottawa RedBlacks on June 25, 2025, is a high-stakes gamble that reeks of desperation. Winnipeg surrendered a 2027 first-round pick and a conditional second-round selection to bring back a player they let walk in free agency just 18 months ago. Brown, who started 10 games for Ottawa in 2024, was traded after a preseason quarterback controversy with Jake Maier. Brown told reporters on July 1 that RedBlacks GM Ryan Dinwiddie cited "arm strength" as the reason for the demotion, a claim Brown disputed. "That's his opinion and he's entitled to that," Brown said. The move leaves Winnipeg with a crowded quarterback room: incumbent Zach Collaros, 36, is under contract through 2026 at a cap hit of $580,000 per season, per 3DownNation. Brown, 27, is signed for 2025 at a base salary of $250,000, but his extension talks are already stalled. The Bombers are now carrying three quarterbacks with starting experience, a luxury that consumes roughly 18% of the salary cap—a risky allocation for a team that went 11-7 in 2024 and lost the West Final.

Oliveira's Homegrown Value vs. Cap Reality

Brady Oliveira remains the emotional and statistical anchor of the Bombers' offense. The Winnipeg native posted his fourth straight 1,000-yard season in 2024, rushing for 1,354 yards and 10 touchdowns while winning the CFL's Most Outstanding Player award. He also moved past Gerry James into fifth place on the franchise's all-time rushing list with 5,702 yards, per BlueBombers.com. "It will always be a special feeling for me," Oliveira said on Canada Day. But Oliveira's contract is a ticking cap bomb. He signed a two-year extension in February 2025 worth $210,000 per season, according to the CFL Transactions Wire. That's a bargain for a star running back, but it expires after 2026, and his next deal—likely north of $250,000 annually—will collide with the Bombers' cap crunch. The team already has 42% of its 2026 cap committed to six players: Collaros, Oliveira, offensive linemen Stanley Bryant and Jermarcus Hardrick, defensive end Willie Jefferson, and linebacker Adam Bighill, per 3DownNation cap analysis. Oliveira's production is undeniable, but his cap hit will force tough cuts elsewhere.

Practice Squad Shuffles and Injury Fallout

The Bombers' transactions wire has been active in July. On July 2, the team signed American defensive back Tyrell Ford to the practice squad, per CFL.ca. Ford, 25, was a 2024 first-round pick of the Edmonton Elks but was released after training camp. He adds depth to a secondary that lost starter Deatrick Nichols to a hamstring injury in Week 6. Nichols is listed as week-to-week, and his absence has exposed a unit that ranks seventh in the CFL in passing yards allowed (298.3 per game). The Bombers also placed Canadian receiver Nic Demski on the six-game injured list on June 28 with a fractured foot. Demski, who had 68 catches for 912 yards in 2024, is a critical loss for an offense that already ranks ninth in net yards per game (342.1). To compensate, Winnipeg signed American receiver Kenny Lawler to a one-year deal on July 1, per the CFL Transactions Wire. Lawler, 30, caught 45 passes for 702 yards with the B.C. Lions in 2024 but was released for cap reasons. His $180,000 salary is a short-term fix, but it pushes the Bombers' 2025 cap spending to $5.2 million, just $200,000 below the league limit, per 3DownNation.

The Canadian Mafia's Draft Dividend

Winnipeg's reliance on Canadian talent remains a competitive advantage. The Bombers earned a supplemental second-round pick in the 2025 CFL Draft for ranking in the top two in Canadian snaps in 2024, per BlueBombers.com. They used that pick to select University of Manitoba offensive lineman Liam McLeod, a 6-foot-5, 310-pound project who will back up Bryant. The team also signed undrafted Canadian linebacker Michael O'Connor out of the University of Guelph to the practice squad on July 3. These moves are cost-effective—McLeod's rookie deal is $65,000 per year—but they underscore a deeper issue: the Bombers are relying on cheap Canadian labor to offset expensive American stars. The strategy worked in 2019 and 2021, but with Collaros aging and the cap tightening, it's a fragile foundation.
