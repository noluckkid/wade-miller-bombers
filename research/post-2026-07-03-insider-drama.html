But the numbers tell a different story. Oliveira has posted four straight 1,000-yard seasons, won Most Outstanding Canadian in 2023 and 2024, and claimed Most Outstanding Player in 2024. Yet his contract, signed in 2023, pays him roughly $210,000 per season—a bargain for his production, but a ticking bomb. With the Bombers committing over 40% of their 2026 cap to six players, including Oliveira, the team is betting its future on a running back who turns 28 this year. A locker room where Oliveira’s public loyalty masks private frustration—a dynamic that could fracture if the Bombers stumble in 2025.

Dru Brown’s Bitter Return: A Quarterback Room on Edge

Dru Brown’s trade back to Winnipeg—for a 2027 first-round pick and a conditional second-rounder—was framed as a homecoming. But his Tuesday practice remarks reveal deeper wounds. Brown confirmed he asked for his release from Ottawa after being benched for Jake Maier, only to be denied. “He said it was an arm strength thing,” Brown said of RedBlacks GM Ryan Dinwiddie. “That’s his opinion.” The subtext is clear: Brown believes he was scapegoated. Now he returns to a Bombers quarterback room where Zach Collaros, 36, is the undisputed starter, but his 2024 numbers—4,300 yards, 24 touchdowns, 15 interceptions—showed decline. Brown’s arrival creates an unspoken competition. Teammates privately note that Brown’s mobility and accuracy in practice have already drawn comparisons to a younger Collaros. The coaching staff, led by O’Shea, insists the depth chart is set, but one source told 3DownNation that “Dru didn’t come back to hold a clipboard.” If Collaros struggles early, the whispers will grow louder—and O’Shea’s loyalty to his veteran could become a wedge.

The Coaching Crossroads: O’Shea’s Grip Slips?

Mike O’Shea, the North Bay, Ontario native, has been the Bombers’ bedrock since 2014, winning Grey Cups in 2019, 2021, and 2023. Pierce, a former quarterback, has pushed for a more pass-heavy attack, while O’Shea favors the run-first identity that made Oliveira a star. The tension boiled over in a closed-door meeting after a Week 6 loss to the Roughriders, where O’Shea reportedly overruled Pierce’s play-calling. Players noticed. “You could feel the energy shift,” one veteran told The Canadian Press. “Coach O’Shea is the boss, but Buck has his own ideas. It’s not a happy medium.” The result? A 2025 offense that ranks 6th in yards per game (345) but 8th in red-zone efficiency (52%). If the Bombers fail to reclaim the West, O’Shea’s job security—once unthinkable—could become a topic. Wade Miller, the president, has publicly backed his coach, but the “Canadian Mafia” ethos only goes so far when the trophy case stays empty.
