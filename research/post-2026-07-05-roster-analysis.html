Blue Bombers' Roster Calculus: Balancing Cap, Youth, and Urgency in a 1-2 Start

The Cap Crunch and the Cost of Continuity

The Blue Bombers entered the 2025 season with one of the CFL’s most top-heavy salary structures. According to league salary data and 3DownNation cap tracking, Winnipeg has committed over 42% of its 2025 salary cap to six players: quarterback Zach Collaros ($550,000), offensive tackle Stanley Bryant ($220,000), defensive end Willie Jefferson ($225,000), running back Brady Oliveira ($210,000), linebacker Adam Bighill ($200,000), and receiver Nic Demski ($185,000). That leaves roughly $3.2 million to fill the remaining 39 active roster spots—a razor-thin margin that limits the team’s ability to make mid-season acquisitions or absorb dead money from cuts.

The Bombers have not made a single significant free-agent signing since February, when they brought in defensive back Jamal Parker on a one-year, $85,000 deal. Instead, general manager Kyle Walters has leaned on the CFL transactions wire for minor depth moves: the signing of American receiver Trey Quinn to the practice roster on June 12, and the addition of Canadian offensive lineman Liam Dobson on June 18 after he was released by the Ottawa Redblacks. These are not impact moves—they are cap-compliant patches.

The consequence is visible on the field. Winnipeg’s run defence ranks seventh in the league, allowing 112.3 yards per game, and the offence has generated only three plays of 30-plus yards through three weeks. The Bombers are paying premium prices for a core that is not producing premium results.

Jaylen Smith and the Youth Movement on Defence

One of the few bright spots in Winnipeg’s transaction ledger is the emergence of second-year linebacker Jaylen Smith. Drafted in the second round (13th overall) of the 2024 CFL Draft out of North Texas, Smith has already surpassed expectations. Through three games, he has recorded 12 defensive tackles, two special-teams tackles, and one quarterback sack—production that has forced defensive coordinator Jordan Younger to expand his role.

Smith’s snap count has risen from 38% in Week 1 to 61% in Week 3, according to CFL statistics. That increase is not accidental: the Bombers placed veteran linebacker Kyrie Wilson on the six-game injured list on June 10 with a hamstring strain, opening a starting spot. Rather than signing a replacement, Walters promoted Smith from the backup rotation. The move saves approximately $45,000 in cap space compared to what a veteran free agent would command.

“I’m just trying to learn, do the job they ask me to do to the best of my ability,” Smith told BlueBombers.com ahead of Winnipeg’s Week 4 game in Hamilton. “I don’t really look at it as upwards until we win a championship.” That attitude is exactly what the Bombers need—a cost-controlled, high-motor player who can grow into a larger role without breaking the cap. Smith’s rookie contract pays him $75,000 in 2025, making him one of the best value-per-snap players on the roster.

Injury Management and the Practice Squad Shuffle

The Bombers’ injury report has been a revolving door in June and July. Beyond Wilson, the team placed starting defensive back Deatrick Nichols on the one-game injured list on June 28 with a knee issue, and receiver Drew Wolitarsky missed Week 3 with a concussion. To compensate, Winnipeg has made four practice roster transactions in the past two weeks: signing American defensive back Tyrell Ford on June 25, adding Canadian receiver Kian Schaffer-Baker on June 27, and promoting defensive lineman Miles Fox to the active roster on June 29.

These moves are telling. Ford, a 2023 CFL Draft pick of the Edmonton Elks who was released in training camp, is a developmental project with no regular-season experience. Schaffer-Baker, a former Saskatchewan Roughriders receiver, has 87 career catches but was available because the Riders deemed him expendable after signing Shawn Bane Jr. Neither player is a proven difference-maker—they are depth pieces acquired on minimum salaries ($70,000 for Ford, $80,000 for Schaffer-Baker).

The Bombers’ practice squad now includes seven players, four of whom are Americans. That ratio is unusual for a team that typically prioritizes Canadian content, but it reflects the reality of a thin market: quality Canadian depth is expensive, and Winnipeg has already allocated its Canadian player budget to starters like Oliveira, Demski, and offensive lineman Chris Kolankowski. The practice squad is where the Bombers are trying to develop low-cost options, but it is also where the cap constraints are most visible—they cannot afford to carry a veteran backup at any position.
